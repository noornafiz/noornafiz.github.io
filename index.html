Profile

The full picture of a man who defies reduction

Nafiz — Lieutenant Colonel Noor Nafiz Islam — was commissioned as a Second Lieutenant in the Bangladesh Army in 2009. In the sixteen years since, the career that began with that commission has expanded in directions most military careers never go. He was promoted to Lieutenant Colonel in 2025 and now commands a high-performance battalion of more than 850 soldiers and civilians — one of the most demanding leadership roles in the Bangladesh military. The responsibility for their readiness, welfare, training, and performance rests with him, every day.

That would be the complete story for most. For Nafiz, it is one chapter of several running simultaneously. He is a published researcher in artificial intelligence, the Internet of Things, and machine learning — with a verified IEEE membership and work presented at international conferences from Dhaka to Linz, Austria. He built and delivered a hospital software system during the COVID-19 pandemic. He served twice under the United Nations flag, in two of the world's most volatile theatres. He has completed four marathons. He has visited approximately thirty countries across four continents. At no point does he appear to have treated any of this as remarkable. That, in itself, is remarkable.

"The label that fits him best is not a rank or a title. He is simply someone who shows up — and then does more than anyone expected."

The photograph on this page was taken on what appears to be an international peacekeeping deployment — African savanna, open horizon, the ease of someone for whom hard places have become familiar. That composure is not performed. It is the natural condition of a man who has been tested in situations most people only read about, enough times that very little unsettles him any more.

Oshamanno Sheba Padak · অসামান্য সেবা পদক · Extraordinary Service Medal

The decoration that is etched into Bangladesh's history

The OSP — Oshamanno Sheba Padak — is one of the most coveted honours in the Bangladesh Armed Forces. It is awarded not for service, but for what an officer does in a crisis when the situation demands more than duty. The current Chief of Army Staff of Bangladesh holds this decoration. It is not given lightly. It is not given often.

Nafiz earned it at Rana Plaza in 2013. On the morning of 24 April, before the dust had settled, before the scale of it was understood, before the orders came — he was already there. Moving through the rubble. The army came later. The operation was formalised later. The chain of command caught up later. What he did in those first hours, and across the three weeks that followed, set the template for everything that came after. One man's movement became the mobilisation of an institution. For that, Bangladesh gave him its highest service decoration. The country does not forget what happened at Rana Plaza. It does not forget who was first.

UN peacekeeping deployment

🇺🇳 United Nations Peacekeeping · Two Deployments · Blue Helmet

I

Democratic Republic of Congo

MONUSCO · UN Organization Stabilization Mission

The DRC hosts one of the largest and most complex UN peacekeeping operations in the world — MONUSCO, operating since 1999 across a country larger than Western Europe, beset by dozens of armed groups, chronic instability, and humanitarian crises compounding across generations. Nafiz served here as a Blue Helmet: one of the soldiers whose presence is the literal difference between a ceasefire holding and collapsing. The work is invisible when it succeeds. He knew that going in. He went in anyway.

II

Sudan — Darfur

UNAMID · AU/UN Hybrid Operation · El Fasher, North Darfur
Characterised as "Hell on Earth"

Darfur is among the most dangerous UN deployment zones on the planet. Since 2003, the region has been defined by mass atrocities, ethnic cleansing, and the displacement of millions of civilians. The UNAMID mission, headquartered in El Fasher, North Darfur, operates in a theatre where the peacekeepers themselves are targeted, where humanitarian corridors collapse without warning, and where the scale of human suffering is genuinely difficult to process from the outside.

Nafiz processed it from the inside. He wore the blue helmet not as a symbol but as a commitment — to stand between violence and the people who deserved protection. To be, in the fullest sense of the word, a preacher of peace in a place that had forgotten what peace felt like. The second tour. The harder one. He volunteered.

Research & Builds

The engineer who builds things that save lives

Nafiz does not separate the act of thinking from the act of building. Every research paper is a system waiting to exist. Every crisis is a design problem.

When COVID-19 hit Bangladesh and the Combined Military Hospital had no platform to manage plasma donors and stored plasma, Nafiz led the development of one — voluntarily, in collaboration with CMH and the Armed Forces Institute of Pathology. The application went into active deployment. Doctors could check plasma availability instantly. Patients received treatment faster. It was built and delivered during a pandemic, by a serving military officer, at no prompting from anyone. That is the kind of initiative that is difficult to teach and impossible to mandate.

His academic work reflects the same instinct toward the applied. A peer-reviewed system for monitoring and managing physical exercise for athletes — using wearable IoT devices — was presented at the 23rd International Conference on Information Integration and Web Intelligence (iiWAS 2021) in Linz, Austria. It is fitting, if on reflection unsurprising, that the researcher who builds athlete monitoring systems is himself a four-time marathon finisher. He also co-authored published research on wearable-based health monitoring for quarantined COVID-19 patients, contributing to a broader roadmap on pandemic-response healthcare technology.

He holds a verified IEEE membership — the world's largest professional engineering body — and his research spans artificial intelligence, the Internet of Things, and machine learning. He conducted and published all of this while simultaneously serving as a Bangladesh Army officer. These are not separate identities. They are facets of the same person.

The Athlete

Running 42 kilometres — four times over

There is a particular discipline required to run a marathon. 42.195 kilometres of nothing but your own legs, lungs, and refusal to stop. Nafiz has done it four times. Not as a hobby. As an expression of the same character he brings to everything else: the conviction that the body — like a battalion, like a research problem, like a rubble site — responds to consistent, relentless effort.

The marathon runner and the peacekeeper are the same person. The uniform changes. The threshold for quitting does not.

2013

Rana Plaza — Oshamanno Sheba Padak (OSP)

Extraordinary Service Medal · Bangladesh Armed Forces

On 24 April 2013, Rana Plaza collapsed in Savar — killing over 1,100 people in the deadliest garment factory disaster in history. In the minutes after, before the sirens, before the operation, before the apparatus of the state had assembled itself around the catastrophe — Nafiz was already in the rubble. The army followed. The rescue operation was built, in part, around the ground he had already covered. Across three weeks, 2,438 people were pulled out alive. The toll reached 1,115 dead. He was there for all of it.

For his conduct across those weeks, he was awarded the Oshamanno Sheba Padak (OSP) — the Extraordinary Service Medal of the Bangladesh Armed Forces. The country reserves this decoration for the moments it needs to remember. Rana Plaza is one of them.
